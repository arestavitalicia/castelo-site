Fourteen Residences. No Two Alike.

Different floor. Different orientation. Different proportions. Different light. Across three buildings and fourteen residences, no two are alike. Each was drawn from scratch, shaped by its position in the complex, its relationship to the garden, the street, the old wall, the sky.

Studios on the ground floor open directly to the private garden. First-floor one-bedrooms, calm and well-proportioned. Upper-floor duplexes with terraces that hold the afternoon light. And crowning the complex, a single three-bedroom duplex at 147 m².

Sizes from 37 to 147 m². Each with its own character. All at the same extraordinary address.

Every home here exists only once.

Castelo suits equally those seeking a primary residence and those making a long-term investment. Setúbal's rising profile, its proximity to Lisbon, and the scarcity of new development in the historic centre combine to make this an address that is unlikely to be replicated.

Where Stone Meets Light

One of a Kind

In Setúbal's historic centre, a residential complex of this scale simply does not exist. Three buildings, fourteen residences, a private garden with a swimming pool, underground parking. Given the nature of the area, it is unlikely to exist again.

Once in a Generation

Castelo occupies the kind of site that appears once in a generation. For all its roots in the past, this is a project designed entirely for the present. Spaces where character and comfort are not in tension, but in agreement.

Old Language, New Life

The architecture speaks the language of the street: traditional volumes, honest materials. Behind the façade, everything is new and built for a life well lived.

A City That Simply Is

Where the River Sado meets the Atlantic, a city has traded in salt, fish, poetry, and secrets for over two thousand years. The Romans knew it. The Moors fortified it. The Portuguese kings fought wars to keep it.

Today, Setúbal is what those who know Portugal well have always known: the coast at its most authentic. Morning markets, fishermen, narrow streets, Arrábida rising behind you like a cathedral of limestone and wild herbs. A fifteen-minute ferry to Tróia, a peninsula so pristine it feels borrowed from another continent.

A place that earns your attention quietly and keeps it forever.

The Wall That Stayed

In 1642, a military engineer named João Gilot drew a fortress around Setúbal. That line became stone. At the western edge, the Baluarte de São Francisco rose to guard one of Portugal's most vital ports.

Nearly four centuries later, the bastion still stands at the back of our land, silent, solid, unmoved. Earthquakes, a tsunami, and centuries of urban growth buried the old defences elsewhere. Here, the wall endured.

Castelo is built alongside that history. The best foundations are the ones already proven by time.
